What this app shows

This is an interactive companion for the proposal "Development of an automated robot-assisted sustainable warehouse system through multi-agent deep reinforcement learning algorithms" (priority area: Manufacturing; TRL 4–6). It demonstrates the proposal's core loop — multiple AGVs learning to coordinate in a shared warehouse — directly in the browser, with the performance indicators named in the proposal: task completion time, storage/retrieval throughput, coordination conflicts, and (for the sustainability objective) energy per order.

Warehouse floor. A 22×13 grid warehouse with shelf blocks, four picking stations and three chargers. Each order is a retrieval: an AGV travels to a shelf face, lifts the pod, delivers to a station, and returns the pod. Three coordination schemes are selectable: (1) independent A* with no coordination — agents plan as if alone and simply wait when blocked; (2) a centralised reservation planner that assigns next-cell reservations by priority, the classical engineered baseline; and (3) the MADRL mode, where A* provides the global route and a learned local policy decides each step from the waypoint direction and neighbour occupancy — the planner–learner hybrid used in recent multi-agent warehouse literature. AGVs consume energy per move and idle tick, and divert to chargers below 25% battery, so congestion shows up directly in the energy KPIs.

Training lab. The MADRL policy is trained live by independent Q-learning (IQL) with a shared tabular Q-function over a compact state (9 waypoint directions × 16 neighbour-occupancy patterns) and 5 actions. Three agents train on a small arena with crossing goals that forces conflicts; learning curves show the mean return rising above the frozen random baseline and conflicts per episode falling. One click deploys the learned table into the floor simulation and the benchmark.

Benchmark. A paired, headless comparison: the same seeded order lists are replayed under all three algorithms, reporting makespan, throughput, conflicts, and energy per order — the exact evaluation design proposed for the simulation study, in miniature.

Honest scope. This is a teaching-scale demonstrator: tabular IQL stands in for deep MADRL (the state is engineered small so it trains in seconds in a browser; the proposal's CTDE/actor–critic and GNN-based methods replace the table with neural networks at scale), the grid dynamics are unit-time and deterministic, and energy figures use nominal per-move costs rather than a measured AGV power model. The structure — shared environment, decentralised execution, paired benchmarking on completion time, collisions and energy — mirrors the proposal's methodology and is intended for demonstrations, teaching, and stakeholder communication.
